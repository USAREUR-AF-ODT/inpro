## What

Dogs and cats from the US enter Germany without quarantine; if paperwork is perfect. Ferrets also allowed with the same rules. Reptiles, birds, exotic species: separate (stricter) process; don't assume they're fine.

The pipeline is deterministic: microchip → rabies vaccine (at least 21 days before travel) → USDA-endorsed EU health certificate issued **within 10 days** of entry → arrival → on-post VTF registration within 30 days.

Plan on **3-4 months of prep time**. Most failures happen because someone skipped one of the timing requirements.

## Required pipeline

### Step 1; ISO-compatible microchip
Your pet must have an **ISO 11784/11785 compliant** 15-digit microchip **before** the rabies vaccine. If the vaccine was given first and the chip later, the rabies series restarts from the chip date. Many US vets implant AVID or 10-digit chips, which are not ISO-compliant. Check the numbers on your paperwork; if it starts with "10" or is 9 digits, you need a new chip or a reader.

### Step 2; Rabies vaccine
- Must be a **killed or recombinant** vaccine (not live)
- Animal must be at least 12 weeks old at vaccination
- Vaccine date must be **at least 21 days before travel**
- If your pet has had continuous yearly rabies boosters, the 21-day wait doesn't apply to boosters; only the first vaccination requires the wait

Keep the vaccine certificate with the vet's signature, date, product name, batch number, and expiration.

### Step 3; USDA-endorsed EU health certificate
This is the paperwork people miss. A standard US health certificate isn't enough; you need the **APHIS-endorsed EU Health Certificate** (Form 7001 + EU attachment, or the newer e-certificate).

1. Schedule an appointment with a USDA-accredited vet **10 days or less before travel** (no more than 10).
2. Bring your pet, the microchip paperwork, rabies certificate, and any recent medical records.
3. The vet examines the pet and completes the EU Health Certificate.
4. The vet either:
   - (preferred, if offered) digitally submits for USDA endorsement through APHIS VEHCS; takes 1-3 business days
   - (old way) ships paperwork to the local USDA APHIS Veterinary Services office for endorsement; takes 5-7 days
5. You receive the endorsed certificate (digital or paper) before travel.

**The 10-day window is hard.** USDA won't endorse a certificate issued more than 10 days before EU entry. Count backward from your actual arrival date in Germany, not your departure from the US.

### Step 4; Flight
Most US→Germany routes work, but **Frankfurt (FRA) and Munich (MUC)** are the main pet entry points with experienced customs staff. You can fly into Ramstein via Patriot Express with pets; there's a special process; coordinate through your losing PCS office.

Cargo pet shipment (via PetSafe on military flights, or via commercial cargo for commercial flights) is often smoother than in-cabin for dogs; fewer surprises at the checkpoint.

### Step 5; Arrival in Germany
At customs, hand over the health certificate. Staff scan the microchip to verify the ID matches. That's it. **No quarantine, no fees, no additional paperwork at the airport**.

### Step 6; Register with the Wiesbaden VTF within 30 days
Pets living on post must register with the Wiesbaden Veterinary Treatment Facility (Bldg 1038, Wurger St., Clay Kaserne). Bring:
- Original health certificate used for import
- Rabies certificate
- Microchip documentation
- SOFA card / dependent ID

Registration is free and mandatory. Pets of personnel living off-post are encouraged but not required to register with the VTF; they'll still need a German civilian vet for primary care (the VTF doesn't do emergency care).

## Quarantine / restrictions in Germany

### Breed restrictions
Hessen (the German state where Wiesbaden sits) has breed-specific legislation. **Pit Bull Terrier, American Staffordshire Terrier, Staffordshire Bull Terrier, Bull Terrier,** and crosses thereof are classified as *Kampfhunde* ("fighting dogs") and require:
- Written permission to enter the state
- Temperament test (Wesenstest) before full ownership approval
- Muzzle and leash in public
- Insurance requirements

Other "dangerous breed" designations (e.g., Rottweiler, Doberman) vary by municipality. Check Stadt Wiesbaden's current rules before committing to a tour with such a breed.

### Leash and tax laws
- Leashes required in public parks and city areas
- Dog tax (*Hundesteuer*) in Wiesbaden: ~€180/year for the first dog, more for additional dogs; fighting breeds taxed higher
- Register your dog with the Stadt Wiesbaden dog registry after Anmeldung

## Gotchas

- **Count the 10-day window from arrival in Germany**, not departure from the US. A 24-hour travel day can push you out of window.
- **"Emotional support" status is not recognized in Germany or under EU pet-travel rules.** Service animals are (with documentation); ESAs are treated as regular pets.
- **Flying in cabin with pets has weight + carrier size limits** that change per airline. Lufthansa's rules are different from United's. Check both legs if you're connecting.
- **Pet-unfriendly leases are common off-post.** Mention pets at your first HSO appointment; narrow the RPP list up front.
- **German civilian vets are excellent** but you pay out-of-pocket (no TRICARE for pets). Pet insurance is common in Germany; consider it for the tour.
- **The VTF does primary care, not emergency care.** For after-hours emergencies, use a civilian 24-hour vet clinic.
- **Rx refills from the VTF require 72-hour lead time.** Don't run your pet out of meds.
- **Pet import from countries OTHER than the US** (if you adopt mid-tour from Germany or another EU country) is a different process; EU Pet Passport.
