## What

You've driven in the US. Germany isn't that different, except when it is. The differences matter: German tickets are expensive, German drivers are less forgiving than American ones, and German rules assume you know them (there are no "forgive the tourist" warnings for the violations that matter).

The USAREUR driver test covers the rules. This page covers the vibe.

## The Autobahn — the famous part

### What you've heard
"No speed limit." Sort of. About **55%** of the Autobahn network has a recommended speed limit of **130 km/h** (~80 mph). The rest has posted limits: 120, 100, 80 depending on road conditions, construction, or local regulation.

In "unrestricted" sections (indicated by the round sign with 4 diagonal slashes over the previous limit), there is no fixed upper speed. You can drive 200+ if the road is clear. The recommended cruising speed is still 130 km/h.

### How it actually works
- **Right lane** is for driving. You cruise in the right lane.
- **Middle lane** is for passing slower traffic.
- **Left lane** is for passing faster traffic, then returning right.
- **Passing on the right is illegal** (unless traffic is jammed and all lanes crawl). You will get honked at, flashed at, and fined for persistent right-lane passing.
- If you're in the left lane and someone flashes their headlights behind you, they're asking you to move right. **Move right.** Don't brake-check them. That's illegal (*Nötigung*) and carries a €350 fine + license points.

### What to do in unrestricted sections
Drive the speed you're comfortable with in the right or middle lane. If you're cruising 130 and a BMW passes you at 220, that's normal. Don't look back; stay in your lane; let them go.

### The Stau (traffic jam)
Germany has a lot of Stau. It's the universal phenomenon. The rules when it happens:
- **Rettungsgasse** — when traffic stops in 2+ lanes, the left lane pulls hard left and right lane pulls hard right, creating a "rescue lane" in the middle for emergency vehicles. **This is mandatory**, not optional. Fine for non-compliance is €200–€320.
- **Keep distance**. Germans tailgate in flowing traffic (uncomfortable for Americans) but in Stau, the standard is tight spacing — don't leave 3 car lengths like you would stateside.

## Speed management — the rules that catch Americans

### Speed cameras everywhere
Germany deploys fixed speed cameras (*Blitzer*) and mobile radar traps extensively. Flash of light in your rearview = ticket in the mail 2–6 weeks later. Fine + photo of you at the wheel.

- **Radar warning apps** (Blitzer.de, Waze, ADAC) are legal for passengers but technically not legal for drivers to use actively. In practice, most people use Waze. At your own discretion.
- **Built-up areas** have 50 km/h default unless posted otherwise
- **Residential streets** often 30 km/h (*Spielstraße*)
- **City centers / pedestrian zones** often 30 km/h or walking speed

### Drinking and driving
- **BAC limit: 0.05%** (stricter than US 0.08)
- **0.0% for under-21 drivers or learner's-permit holders**
- **Zero tolerance for visible impairment** — you can be arrested below 0.05 if you drive erratically
- **License suspension + fine** for ≥0.05; arrest for ≥0.11

German beer is stronger than US beer (averaging 5-6% vs 4-5%). Two beers is often over the limit. **Don't drive after drinking.** Taxis, Deutsche Bahn, or stay over.

### Phone use
- **Hand-held phone while driving**: €100 fine + 1 point
- **Hands-free (speaker / Bluetooth)**: legal
- **Texting even at a red light with engine on**: illegal. Engine off = technically OK but not practical.

### Winter tires
- **Nov 1 – Apr 15**: winter tires (or all-weather labeled M+S/Alpine snowflake) required **when driving conditions are wintry** (snow, ice, slush). Not date-based — weather-based. If it snows in October, you need them then.
- **Ticket for not having them** in winter conditions: €60–€120 + license points
- **All-season tires** with both M+S and Alpine snowflake symbol comply

## The German driving style

- **Lane discipline is strict.** Stay in the right lane unless actively passing.
- **Turn signals are required** not just courteous. No signal = fine.
- **Priority from the right (*Rechts vor Links*)** at uncontrolled intersections: the car on the right has right of way. Unless signs say otherwise.
- **Roundabouts**: car in the circle has priority. Newcomers slow the flow — stay aware.
- **Zebra crosswalks (Zebrastreifen)**: pedestrians ALWAYS have priority. Don't roll through.
- **Bike lanes cross driveways**: bikes have priority. Scan before pulling out.
- **School buses with flashing lights**: don't pass, both directions (same as US).

**Points add up**. Get 8 points in your 2-year window and USAREUR pulls your Certificate of License. Germans lose their Führerschein at 8. The ADAC "Punktekonto" website shows current point totals.

## The ADAC card (German AAA)

**ADAC** is the German equivalent of AAA. Benefits:
- 24/7 roadside assistance (Pannenhilfe)
- Towing included
- European-wide coverage
- Annual fee ~€60–€90
- Comprehensive paperwork assistance for vehicle issues

**Worth it** if you drive frequently in Germany or plan European road trips. Sign up at the ADAC Wiesbaden office (or online).

## Fuel

- **Super** (95 octane) — standard US gas equivalent for older cars
- **Super Plus** (98 octane) — premium
- **Diesel** — far more common in Germany than US; most German-made cars are diesel
- **Bleifrei** — unleaded (they all are now, but the label persists)

Your US-spec car takes Super or Super Plus, NOT diesel. **Putting diesel in a gas car is a €2,000+ mistake**. Read the pump.

### Price: €1.70/L (~$7/gal) at regular stations. Shoppette (on-post) is ~$3.50/gal. Worth the detour for most fills.

## Gotchas

- **Parking is expensive off-post.** Wiesbaden parking garages: €2.50/hour. Metered street parking: €1–€3/hour, 1–3 hour max. Residential-only zones enforced by Politesse.
- **Don't park blocking a driveway.** German neighbors WILL call the Politesse. Fine + tow.
- **Autobahn tolls**: Germany doesn't charge passenger-car tolls on its Autobahn (yet). Trucks pay. Many other European countries do — buy a toll sticker (Vignette) for Austria, Switzerland, Czech Republic before entering.
- **Crossing into France / Benelux / Poland** has no toll stickers; highway tolls are pay-as-you-go, mostly credit card at booths.
- **Accident protocol**: call 110 (Polizei) and 112 (medical/fire) as appropriate. Exchange insurance info. **Don't admit fault** at the scene — insurance sorts it. Take photos.
- **DUI under SOFA status**: reported to your command AND to German authorities. Career-ending.
- **German parking tickets** must be paid within 14 days via bank transfer to the Polizei. If you leave one unpaid, it follows you through Anmeldung records.
