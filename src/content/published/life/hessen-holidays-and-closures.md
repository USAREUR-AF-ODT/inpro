## What

Germany operates on a layered holiday calendar: **federal (*bundesweit*) holidays** are observed nationwide; each state (*Bundesland*) adds its own **state holidays**. **Hessen**; the state Wiesbaden sits in; adds **Fronleichnam** (Corpus Christi) and does **not** observe Reformation Day (which other states do).

Stores close on Sundays and public holidays under the **Ladenschlussgesetz** (shop-closing law). Restaurants, gas stations, pharmacies (on-call duty), on-post facilities, and essential services keep working. Planning around this is a quality-of-life issue: if Friday is a holiday and Sunday is already closed, Thursday's grocery run needs to cover 3 days.

## Where

Holiday observance is **where you are**, not where you come from. SOFA status doesn't exempt you from Sunday closures in Wiesbaden. Stores in military-installation gates (Commissary, PX, Shoppette) follow **US and on-post operational schedules**, not the German Ladenschlussgesetz; so they may open when off-post stores are closed, or vice versa.

## When

### Federal holidays (observed everywhere in Germany)
- **Neujahr**; January 1 (New Year's Day)
- **Karfreitag**; Friday before Easter (Good Friday)
- **Ostermontag**; Easter Monday
- **Tag der Arbeit**; May 1 (Labor Day)
- **Christi Himmelfahrt**; 40 days after Easter (Ascension Day); also Father's Day informally
- **Pfingstmontag**; Whit Monday, 50 days after Easter
- **Tag der Deutschen Einheit**; October 3 (German Unity Day)
- **1. Weihnachtstag**; December 25 (Christmas Day)
- **2. Weihnachtstag**; December 26 (St. Stephen's / Boxing Day)

### Hessen-specific state holidays
- **Fronleichnam**; Corpus Christi, 60 days after Easter (May/June); **Hessen observes; not all states do**

### NOT observed in Hessen
- **Reformation Day** (October 31); observed in several other states but not Hessen
- **All Saints' Day** (November 1); observed in Bavaria, NRW, etc. but not Hessen
- **Assumption of Mary** (August 15); observed in Saarland and parts of Bavaria
- **Buß- und Bettag** (Day of Repentance, mid-November); observed only in Saxony

### Dates that shift by year
Most Christian holidays (Easter cluster, Pentecost cluster, Corpus Christi) move yearly. Memorize the **Easter anchor**: Easter is the first Sunday after the first full moon after the spring equinox. Once you know Easter, every other moving holiday is a fixed offset.

## Ladenschlussgesetz; the shop-closing rules

German law restricts when commercial stores can be open. Key rules:

- **Monday-Saturday**: stores can open 0600-2200
- **Sundays and holidays**: commercial stores must be **closed**
- **Exceptions**: grocery stores at airports, train stations, and gas stations can open Sundays (limited inventory)
- **Special Sundays** (*verkaufsoffene Sonntage*): municipalities can authorize 4 Sundays per year for Sunday shopping; Wiesbaden uses some around Christmas markets and seasonal events

### What stays open Sundays and holidays
- **Gas stations** (tankstellen); snacks, drinks, limited groceries
- **Bakeries**; many open Sunday mornings, closed afternoons
- **Restaurants and cafés**; most open
- **Pharmacies**; always a rotating on-call pharmacy (*Notdienst*); check the window sign for the nearest open one
- **Hospitals and emergency services**; always
- **Train-station shops and airport shops**; grocery items available
- **On-post Shoppette and Commissary**; follow their own schedule
- **Christmas markets** in December; open Sundays during the Advent season

### What's closed
- Grocery stores (Rewe, Edeka, Aldi, Lidl, Netto)
- Drugstores (dm, Rossmann, Müller)
- Most retail (clothing, electronics, home goods)
- Banks (though ATMs work)
- Post offices (though Paketshops in grocery stores are closed too, but DHL lockers work)
- Hair salons, barbers
- Auto repair (some emergency service available)

## Planning your week

- **Thursday evenings** are peak grocery nights. Stores are open late; Germans stock up because Saturday is busy and Sunday is closed.
- **Saturday mornings**; markets thrive; Wiesbaden's Samstagsmarkt (Mauritiusplatz or Dern'sches Gelände) runs weekly.
- **Holiday weeks with bridge days** (*Brückentage*); when a holiday falls Tuesday or Thursday, many Germans take Monday/Friday as "bridge" vacation. Expect empty offices and peak vacation travel.
- **Trash pickup** shifts around holidays. Monday trash often moves to Tuesday after a Monday holiday. Check the Hainerberg/Aukamm recycling calendars on home.army.mil/wiesbaden.
- **Bank transactions** on Friday before a holiday may not clear until Tuesday. Plan €500 cash for holiday weekends.
- **Medical appointments**; doctors close for holidays; urgent care falls to emergency rooms (*Notaufnahme*). See [Emergency numbers](/entries/medical/emergency-numbers).

## Notable cultural events tied to holidays

- **Karneval / Fasching**; pre-Lent carnival, late January through Ash Wednesday. Wiesbaden has a parade; Mainz's is larger (one of Germany's big three carnivals). Schools and many workplaces have early dismissal on *Rosenmontag* (Rose Monday).
- **Walpurgisnacht**; April 30 night; traditional bonfire celebrations especially in the Taunus mountains.
- **Mai-Baum**; May 1; villages raise a decorated tree. Many communities hold Maifest celebrations.
- **Erntedank**; Thanksgiving equivalent in early October; church services, local markets with harvest decorations.
- **St. Martin**; November 11; kids' lantern parades (*Laternenumzug*). Big deal in kindergartens and elementary schools.
- **Nikolaus**; December 6; children put boots outside the door at night for St. Nicholas to leave treats.
- **Heiligabend (Christmas Eve)**; December 24; stores close by 1400, public transport drops service by 1600. Gift exchange happens in the evening, not Christmas morning.
- **Silvester**; December 31; heavy fireworks at midnight, sold at grocery stores in late December. Wiesbaden bans fireworks in the city center in some years.

## Gotchas

- **The first Sunday after arrival** catches everyone off guard. Stock groceries Saturday afternoon. Don't assume "I'll grab something tomorrow."
- **Karfreitag is the strictest holiday of the year.** "Quiet day" (*stiller Feiertag*) law restricts loud music, public dancing, and some commercial activity beyond normal holiday rules. Plan accordingly.
- **Bridge-day traffic** on highways is brutal. If a Thursday holiday has a Friday bridge, leave your trip on Wednesday evening or Saturday morning.
- **Pharmacy Notdienst** is posted in the window of every pharmacy; also searchable at aponet.de. Don't assume the closest is open; follow the rotation.
- **On-post Shoppette is a lifeline** on Sundays. Limited inventory but carries the essentials.
- **Tankstellen shops overpay**; small markup on Sunday purchases, but they're open. Fine for one-off emergencies; not a grocery replacement.
- **German Christmas markets open late November**, close around December 23. Missing the window means no Glühwein until next year.
- **Don't schedule PCS inbound travel for a federal holiday.** In-processing offices are closed; Lodge may be slow; your sponsor may not be around.
- **Kids' school holidays** (*Ferien*) are separate from public holidays. DoDEA runs its own calendar; German public-school kids have different breaks (Hessen publishes annually). Plan family logistics against both.
- **The "quiet hours" rule** (Sunday rest, *Ruhezeit*) means no lawn mowers, no drilling, no loud vacuuming Sundays and most weekday evenings after 2200. Germans will tell you; sometimes sharply; if you violate this.
