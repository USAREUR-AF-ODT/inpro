## What

Most military families in Wiesbaden run a two-account or three-account system:

1. **A US-based bank** (USAA, Navy Federal Credit Union, Andrews Federal Credit Union, or a regional US bank); receives DFAS direct-deposit pay, holds US savings, handles US bill pay
2. **Community Bank**; a DoD-contracted bank with branches on most OCONUS installations; offers dual US-domiciled accounts with easy on-post cash access, check-writing, and direct connection to USAREUR payroll
3. **A German bank**; optional; needed if your landlord requires SEPA direct debit from a German IBAN, or if you have recurring German obligations that ACH/card won't handle

See [Banking options](/entries/finance/banking-options) for the topline comparison; this entry is the deeper mechanics of how each piece fits.

## Where

### US military-focused banks
- **USAA**; online-only, serves military families worldwide, excellent customer service, no physical branches
- **Navy Federal Credit Union (NFCU)**; has OCONUS branches (none on Clay Kaserne but has one at Ramstein); ATMs at Ramstein and other major installations
- **Andrews Federal Credit Union**; branches on Clay Kaserne and Hainerberg; widely used at Wiesbaden
- **Pentagon Federal Credit Union**; strong CD/savings rates; some military-focused features

### On-post
- **Community Bank (Clay Kaserne)**; the DoD-contracted bank; physically on Clay
- **Andrews FCU branches**; both Clay and Hainerberg
- **AAFES Exchange**; cashiers at Exchange counters sometimes cash checks

### Off-post German banks
- **Nassauische Sparkasse** (the Sparkasse Wiesbaden); regional public savings bank; many branches and ATMs
- **Deutsche Bank**; national; fewer branches but robust
- **Commerzbank**; national
- **Targobank**; national, retail focus
- **DKB, ING Germany, Comdirect, N26**; online-only banks, German IBAN without physical branches

## When

- **Community Bank account opening**; walk-in during duty hours; typically same-day account open
- **USAA / Navy Federal / Andrews FCU**; online account opening; can be done pre-arrival from CONUS
- **German bank account opening**; typically 1-2 weeks; requires Anmeldung and a local address

Contact details in the frontmatter. For specific situations:

## US bank strategy

### DFAS pay goes here
Most Active Duty soldiers route DFAS LES direct-deposit to their US bank (USAA, NFCU, Andrews). This keeps pay in USD, avoids conversion losses on each pay cycle, and simplifies tax reporting. Don't use a German bank for DFAS direct-deposit; the conversion each month costs you on rate and fees.

### Why USAA for many
- No monthly fees for core checking
- ATM-fee reimbursement up to ~$15/month for out-of-network withdrawals
- Strong fraud detection
- OCONUS-aware customer service (won't auto-flag your German purchases as fraud)
- Deploy-friendly tools (bill pay from theater, etc.)

### Why Navy Federal for many
- Broader product range (mortgages for PCS-out, auto loans, investment)
- Physical branches at some installations
- More competitive rates on certain products

### Why Andrews FCU at Wiesbaden
- **Physical branch on Clay Kaserne and Hainerberg**; walk-in banking
- Same-day check deposit
- Integrated with Community Bank for some functions

**Many families use two**: USAA for pay + bill pay + fraud-resistant travel; Andrews or NFCU for branch access and savings products.

## Community Bank; what it's for

Community Bank is operated by **Bank of America Overseas Military Banking Overseas (OMBO)** contract. Purposes:

- **Cash Euros on-post**; you withdraw USD from US accounts, convert at Community Bank, get Euros; sometimes better rate than off-post ATMs
- **Check cashing**; accepts US checks for families who still write them (less common)
- **Specialized OCONUS products**; military-family-aware products like deployment savings, temporary allotments
- **Wire transfers** to German banks for rent deposits; easier than initiating from a CONUS bank

Not everyone needs Community Bank. If your US bank handles everything and you don't need on-post cash/check services, you can skip it.

## German bank strategy

### Do you actually need one?
**No** for most families who live on-post, pay commissary/PX in USD, and don't have German rental obligations.

**Yes** for families who:
- Live off-post with a German landlord who requires SEPA rent (most common reason)
- Have recurring German bills (utility companies that only take SEPA direct debit)
- Want to join a gym, club, or subscription service that won't accept US cards
- Earn income in Germany (working spouse, contractor)

### Which German bank
- **Sparkasse Wiesbaden (Naspa)**; most convenient for Wiesbaden residents; public savings bank with many branches and ATMs; English-speaking staff common at main branch
- **Deutsche Bank**; fine, more CONUS-like banking experience
- **ING Germany or Comdirect**; online-only; fewer branches but no/low fees
- **N26**; Berlin-based neobank; popular with expats; English-first app; uses German IBAN

### How to open a German bank account
1. **Complete Anmeldung** first; you'll need the Anmeldebestätigung
2. **Gather documents**: passport, SOFA card, Anmeldebestätigung, proof of German address
3. **Visit the branch** (for Sparkasse/Deutsche Bank); 30-60 min appointment
4. **Online application** (for N26, ING, Comdirect); takes 1-2 weeks with video ID
5. **Receive EC-Karte (debit card)** and **IBAN** within 5-10 business days
6. **Set up SEPA direct debit** for rent, utilities, etc.

### Common pitfalls at German banks
- **Contracts with monthly fees** are the default at Sparkasse and Deutsche Bank; €3-10/month for checking
- **Overdraft (Dispokredit)** can be approved automatically; interest rates are steep
- **SCHUFA** (German credit agency) pulls happen at account opening; low credit = limited products
- **English capability** at smaller Sparkasse branches varies; Innenstadt branch is better than outlying ones

## SEPA and IBAN mechanics

- **IBAN** (International Bank Account Number); a long string starting with DE for German banks
- **SEPA** (Single Euro Payments Area); the EU-wide bank-transfer network; free and near-instant between SEPA members
- **SEPA direct debit**; authorizes a business to pull money from your account; the German default for rent, utilities, insurance, subscriptions
- **Your US bank will not accept SEPA direct debit**; this is why some families need a German bank

### Transferring from US bank to German bank
- **Wise (TransferWise)**; 0.5% fee typically, real exchange rate, 1-2 day transfer; the go-to for most families
- **Your US bank**; often 1-3% fee with worse exchange rate; convenient but costly
- **Community Bank**; specialized OCONUS rates; may be competitive for larger transfers
- **Western Union, MoneyGram**; emergency cash to hand; expensive

For regular rent payments, set up a recurring Wise transfer that lands in the German account 3-5 days before rent is due.

## Credit cards for overseas use

- **No-foreign-transaction-fee cards** are mandatory. USAA, Chase Sapphire, Capital One, Amex Gold/Platinum all offer such cards.
- **Contactless tap-to-pay** is standard in Germany (including at grocery stores); older cards without contactless can frustrate checkout.
- **Chip-and-PIN vs chip-and-signature**; Germany is chip-and-PIN heavy; some US signature-only cards fail at automated kiosks (train ticket machines, gas pump pay-at-pump, parking garages). Test your card; keep cash backup.
- **Amex acceptance** is lower than Visa/Mastercard in Germany. Bring both.
- **Apple Pay / Google Pay** widely accepted at German retailers; often more reliable than physical card at automated kiosks.

## Deployment banking

See the [SDP deployment savings](/entries/finance/sdp-deployment-savings) entry for the Savings Deposit Program specifically. Other deployment considerations:

- **Power of attorney** for the at-home spouse on joint accounts
- **Deployment savings accounts** (USAA, NFCU) for tax-free combat pay deposits
- **Bill pay automation**; set up while still in garrison so nothing falls through the cracks
- **Card travel notices**; notify your bank of deployment destination to avoid fraud flags
- **Communicate with US tax preparer** early if deployment crosses tax filing windows

## Gotchas

- **Don't route DFAS pay to a German bank account.** Currency conversion at each pay cycle eats 1-3% and the exchange isn't in your favor.
- **USAA's fee reimbursement** has a monthly cap. Heavy ATM users (cash-heavy spenders) exceed it.
- **German bank SCHUFA pulls**; open your German account **before** applying for a rental agreement; landlords also want SCHUFA reports. Order yours ahead.
- **EC-Karte vs Girocard**; German debit cards work at German businesses; many are NOT internationally compatible without a Maestro logo. Keep a credit card for travel.
- **Cash still matters in Germany.** 30% of transactions are cash; Apotheken, small cafés, farmer's markets prefer cash. Keep €100-300 on hand.
- **ATM withdrawal limits**; both US and German banks have daily caps (~$500 typical). Planning large cash needs (rental deposit, car purchase) means multiple days of withdrawals or a wire transfer.
- **Counter-service at US banks via phone**; international calls cost. Many USAA/NFCU numbers have international-access lines that are free or reduced-cost.
- **German bank statements** come by mail unless you opt for electronic. Opt for electronic from day one.
- **Joint accounts vs. individual accounts**; German banks treat joint accounts differently; discuss with banker.
- **Your SOFA status** doesn't exempt you from German bank KYC (know-your-customer) requirements. Bring all documents.
- **PCS-out bank closure**; give 30+ days notice for German banks to avoid recurring-billing chaos. Close after the final rent and utility bills clear.
- **US checks are rare in Germany.** Your German landlord won't take a US check; plan to wire or SEPA.
