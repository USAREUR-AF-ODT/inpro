## What

Wiesbaden is a mid-to-high cost-of-living German city — wealthier than Kaiserslautern or Grafenwoehr, cheaper than Munich or Frankfurt city center. Expect it to feel 10–20% more expensive than an average US mid-Atlantic suburb day-to-day, offset by allowances designed to equalize.

Three allowances interact:
- **OHA** — Overseas Housing Allowance; reimburses rent up to a rank-specific cap
- **COLA** — Cost of Living Allowance; daily stipend reflecting local grocery/service prices vs. CONUS
- **TLA** — Temporary Lodging Allowance; covers the 10–60 day gap between landing and permanent quarters

All three are paid in USD on your LES. None are taxed.

## Real numbers (rough, late 2024)

### Rent (pays through OHA)
- 2-bedroom off-post apartment in Wiesbaden proper: €1,400–€2,200/month
- 3-bedroom house in Taunusstein or Bierstadt: €1,800–€2,800/month
- On-post: no rent; utilities bundled

OHA caps (rounded, vary by rank):
- E-5 w/dependents: $2,000/month
- O-3 w/dependents: $2,700
- O-5 w/dependents: $3,200

OHA reimburses **actual rent up to the cap**. You pocket nothing if your rent is below cap; you cover any overage.

### Utilities (on-post, bundled)
Included in your on-post quarters. If you live off-post:
- Gas heating (*Erdgas*): €80–€150/month in winter, €40–€80 summer
- Electricity: €80–€140/month (Germany has high electricity rates)
- Water: €30–€60/month
- Internet (DSL or fiber): €30–€50/month
- Mobile phone (German SIM): €15–€40/month depending on data
- **Rundfunkbeitrag (TV tax)**: €18.36/month, mandatory off-post

Most utilities are **billed annually** with monthly prepayments, plus a year-end settlement (Jahresabrechnung) that can be a refund or a surprise bill.

### Groceries
Commissary prices ≈ US prices + 5% surcharge. German grocery prices higher than the commissary for most items, but better quality produce/bread.

Budget ~$600–$900/month for a family of four combining commissary + off-post specialty.

### Restaurants
- Casual sit-down: €15–€25/person
- Mid-range (Italian, Asian, German): €25–€40/person
- Nice restaurant with wine: €50–€80/person
- Tipping: round up 5–10% (not 20% — waitstaff are salaried)

### Transportation
- Gas (off-post): €1.70/L (~$7/gal USD)
- Gas (Shoppette, tax-free): ~$3.50/gal USD
- Monthly Deutschlandticket (unlimited regional rail): €58/month (price updated 2024-2025)
- Single U-Bahn/bus ticket: €3.20
- Taxi: €3.50 base + ~€2.50/km

### Childcare
- CYS on-post (sliding scale by rank): $200–$800/month depending
- German Kita (public daycare): €100–€400/month income-based, typically not available before age 3
- Private Kita or private nanny: €800–€1,500/month
- International preschool: €800–€1,500/month

### Education
- DoDEA schools: free for ADFMs
- Off-post German public schools: free
- International schools (Frankfurt International, Metropolitan): €15,000–€25,000/year

## How COLA affects the monthly

COLA is paid on **LES monthly** based on the cost index for Wiesbaden vs. CONUS. It fluctuates with EUR/USD exchange rate and local inflation. Recent years have seen COLA trending down as the USD strengthened.

Don't count on COLA to cover shortfalls — budget as if it were $0 and treat it as a cushion.

## The transition-month crunch

The killer expense is your first month off-post:
- **Deposit**: 2–3 months rent (refundable at end of lease; German tenants expect this)
- **First month rent**: paid up front
- **Utility setup deposits**: €50–€200 per utility (some non-refundable)
- **Rundfunkbeitrag**: billed quarterly after first month

For a €1,800/month 2-bedroom, that's ~**€6,000–€8,000 out of pocket** in Week 3. OHA reimbursement lags — you pay the landlord, they receive reimbursement 4–6 weeks later in USD, you cover the gap.

Plan for **$10,000 USD liquid available** before you sign a lease, or arrange an AER (Army Emergency Relief) bridge loan through your unit or ACS.

## What saves money

- **Stay on-post** — no rent, no deposit, no utility setup
- **VAT forms** — 19% off most purchases; see VAT Forms entry. Over a year of grocery + furniture + misc: $2K–$5K savings
- **Deutschlandticket** instead of driving — if one spouse doesn't commute, skip a second car
- **Commissary + Shoppette** for groceries and fuel — cost delta vs. off-post is real
- **Free CYS programs** (SKIES, sports) — quality is high, subsidized heavily

## What costs more than expected

- **Eating out** — frequent eating in Wiesbaden drains the COLA fast
- **Traveling during holidays** — Christmas markets are charming but hotel prices triple; Oktoberfest is astronomical
- **Imported US goods** — Amazon.de sometimes ships from the EU cheaper than AAFES online; sometimes not. Check.
- **Gas for extensive European travel** — those Autobahn road trips add up at €1.70/L
- **Children's activities** — German swim classes, sports clubs, music lessons all add up; many are subsidized for residents

## Gotchas

- **Utility year-end settlement** can hit you with a €500–€1,500 Nachzahlung (owed payment) if the monthly prepayments were too low. Or a refund if overpaid. Review the Mietvertrag estimate carefully.
- **Renter's insurance (Hausratversicherung)** is expected by most landlords — ~€80–€150/year. Cheap; buy it.
- **Private liability insurance (Haftpflichtversicherung)** is strongly recommended — German neighbors sue for spilled red wine on the carpet. ~€50–€100/year. Worth it.
- **OHA is not an advance.** You pay the German landlord, reimbursement follows. Budget accordingly.
- **Rundfunk kicks in at Anmeldung.** Skipping it is not legal; they find you.
- **Vehicle tax (Kfz-Steuer)** for non-USAREUR-plated cars — you probably don't have this if you're on USAREUR plates, but a German-plated car costs €100–€500/year.
- **Moving costs at PCS-out** are partially covered by DTS, but transporting anything bought in Germany to CONUS may exceed your shipping weight allowance. Plan downsizing.
