## What

The TL;DR for newcomers: keep your US bank, open one on-post US account (Andrews or Community), and open one German account for SEPA bank transfers (the German equivalent of ACH). Most things can be paid from any of these, but each does one thing better.

## The three banking layers

### Your existing US bank
Keep it. USAA, Navy Federal, your credit union — whatever you were using in CONUS works here. Use it for:
- US bill autopay (US credit cards, loans, investments)
- Debit card withdrawals from German ATMs (watch foreign transaction fees)
- Receiving your LES paycheck if you don't want to switch accounts

USAA and Navy Federal have the best foreign exchange rates among US banks. Big-name banks (Chase, BofA, Wells Fargo) charge more. Check your bank's international ATM fee schedule before you land.

### On-post US bank — pick one
Two options, both with branches on Clay Kaserne:

**Andrews Federal Credit Union**
- Branches on post and at multiple USAREUR installations
- SDP (Savings Deposit Program) enrollment during deployment
- Good customer service for SOFA-status specifics (car loans, etc.)
- Debit cards that work in both US and German ATMs

**Community Bank**
- DoD-contracted bank serving overseas installations
- Checking + savings in USD
- Limited German-side functionality (intentionally — you're supposed to use a German bank for day-to-day euros)

Pick one. Opening an account requires: CAC, orders, and about 20 minutes. Many use Community for USD checking + Andrews for credit cards; some pick one and stay with it. Both work.

### German bank — you need one
For rent (OHA reimburses you in USD but you pay your landlord in EUR via SEPA), utilities, Rundfunkbeitrag, mobile phone plans, grocery auto-delivery — almost everything off-post runs on SEPA direct debit from a German IBAN.

Options:

**Sparkasse Wiesbaden** — the local municipal bank. Branches everywhere. Branch staff handle US customers regularly. Physical debit card (EC-Karte / Girocard). Small monthly account fee (~€5). No English online banking UI but the mobile app handles most things. **Best fit if you want a local relationship and don't mind German paperwork.**

**Commerzbank / Deutsche Bank** — national banks. Similar to Sparkasse but larger footprint. Higher fees, more English support. **Best fit if you travel often across Germany.**

**N26 or Revolut** (online-only) — app-based, German IBAN, instant signup, English-first UI, no monthly fees on basic tier. Debit cards work everywhere. **Best fit if you want zero-friction setup and don't need branch banking.** Most junior arrivees go this route.

### What can't you do with just US banks?

- Pay rent to a German landlord (US banks can't originate SEPA; wire transfers are expensive)
- Receive your German utility bills direct-debit (required by most utility companies)
- Pay Rundfunkbeitrag without penalties (they want SEPA only)
- Pay many German online retailers that don't accept foreign cards

## How to open a German bank account

### Sparkasse / traditional
Bring to the branch:
1. Passport or German residence card
2. **Proof of address in Germany** — this is where Anmeldung matters. Your *Anmeldebescheinigung* (registration confirmation) is what they want.
3. Orders or SOFA-status paper
4. €25–50 minimum initial deposit

You'll walk out with an IBAN and a temporary card. The permanent EC-Karte arrives by mail in 5–10 days, PIN separately.

### N26 / Revolut
1. Download the app
2. Video-verify with your passport
3. Address: your German address post-Anmeldung works; many accept Army Lodge or on-post address
4. Account live in 1–3 days; debit card mailed within a week

## Currency strategy

- **Paycheck / LES** → US bank (USAA or on-post)
- **Move EUR for rent** → ACH from US bank to Andrews → wire to German bank, or use Wise (cheapest FX)
- **Pay rent in EUR** → German bank SEPA direct-debit to landlord
- **Daily spend** → German debit card (no foreign transaction fee because it's a German card)
- **US online purchases** → US credit card

**Wise** (formerly TransferWise) is often the best tool for moving USD↔EUR. The cost is typically 0.4% above mid-market; US banks charge 2–4% on top of a worse rate.

## Gotchas

- **Your on-post US debit card may NOT work in German ATMs** — test it within the first week. If it doesn't, you need a German debit card for cash access off-post.
- **Germany is still cash-heavy.** Many small restaurants, bakeries, and street markets don't take cards. Keep €50–100 cash on you.
- **Credit cards are less accepted than in the US.** Girocard (EC-Karte) and cash are the default. Amex is rarely accepted. Visa/MC more so. Get a German debit card.
- **Rundfunkbeitrag direct-debits automatically** once you register your household. Don't close your German bank account mid-tour without updating them first, or they'll escalate to collections fast.
- **Financial Readiness Program (FRP) at ACS** offers free 1-on-1 budget counseling — use it in your first 30 days if you're new to European banking.
- **Credit history doesn't transfer.** Your US FICO is invisible here. If you want a German credit card, expect a secured-card setup first.
