## What

Renting a German apartment is structurally different from renting in the US:

- **Long-term by default**: contracts typically 1-3 years, sometimes indefinite with 3-month notice
- **You provide the furniture** (most of the time); empty means empty: no fridge, no stove, no lights, sometimes no kitchen
- **Tenant-friendly law**: it's easier to stay than to get evicted
- **Significant upfront costs**: 2-3 months deposit + first month + setup fees
- **Paperwork intensive**: expect to hand over ID copies, income proof, and a SCHUFA credit check

If you're going through HSO + RPP (Rental Partnership Program), HSO vets these things. If you're going off the approved list (which isn't advised), you navigate this yourself.

## The search

### Where apartments are listed
- **Immobilienscout24** (immobilienscout24.de); the major German rental site
- **Immowelt** (immowelt.de); second-largest
- **Kleinanzeigen** (kleinanzeigen.de); Craigslist equivalent; lots of scams
- **Wiesbadener Kurier classifieds**; local paper, rare but sometimes gems
- **HSO RPP list**; vetted landlords; the main path for US military

### Filters worth using
- **Kaltmiete** (cold rent) up to your OHA cap
- **Warmmiete** (warm rent = cold + utilities) as actual budget ceiling
- **Rooms** (German = living/bedroom count; 3-Zimmer is 3 rooms total, not 3 bedrooms)
- **Area** (Wohnfläche) in m²; 1 m² ≈ 10.76 sq ft

### Key German terms
- **Wohnung**: apartment
- **Haus**: house
- **Zimmer**: room (counts living + bedrooms)
- **Küche**: kitchen (may or may not include appliances)
- **Bad**: bathroom
- **WG**: Wohngemeinschaft (shared flat)
- **EBK**: Einbauküche (built-in kitchen; good, means you get appliances)
- **Möbliert**: furnished (rare for family rentals)
- **Unmöbliert**: unfurnished
- **Kaltmiete**: base rent (no utilities)
- **Warmmiete**: rent + utilities
- **Nebenkosten**: additional utility costs (water, heating, building maintenance)
- **Kaution**: security deposit
- **Provision**: realtor's commission (illegal for tenant to pay in most cases since 2015; landlord pays realtor)
- **Mietvertrag**: rental contract
- **Staffelmiete**: contractually-scheduled rent increases
- **Indexmiete**: rent pegged to inflation index
- **Zeitmietvertrag**: fixed-term lease

## The math

### Kaltmiete vs Warmmiete
A listing at **"€1,400 kalt"** is base rent. **"Plus Nebenkosten ~€250"** makes it €1,650/month all-in (Warmmiete). If the listing is **"€1,600 warm,"** that's all-in including Nebenkosten.

### Nebenkosten breakdown
Typical monthly prepayment for a 2-bedroom in Wiesbaden:
- Heating (oil or gas): €80-150
- Water + sewage: €30-60
- Building common-area electricity: €20-40
- Trash + building maintenance: €30-60
- Property tax share (Grundsteuer): €10-30
- Insurance (Gebäudeversicherung): €5-15

**Total**: €175-350/month typical. You prepay monthly; annual settlement (Nebenkostenabrechnung) reconciles actual vs. estimated.

### Upfront at lease signing
- **Kaution (deposit)**: 2-3 months Kaltmiete, held in separate account
- **First month rent**: prorated
- **Setup fees**: €50-200 for utility hookups (gas, electric, water)
- **Landlord admin** (rare): some charge a small admin fee

For €1,400/mo Kaltmiete: **€4,200-5,600 upfront** just for move-in. Plan accordingly.

### OHA covers...
- Kaltmiete up to your rank's cap (not Nebenkosten in most cases; those come from your pocket)
- Some utility components (heat, water) via OHA
- NOT Rundfunkbeitrag, internet, personal electric

## The application process

### 1. Visit (Besichtigung)
You see the apartment. Landlord or Makler (realtor) present. Bring:
- Copy of passport/SOFA card
- Copy of orders (for income proof)
- References from previous landlords if available
- SCHUFA credit check (German equivalent of credit score)

### 2. SCHUFA credit check
SCHUFA is Germany's credit bureau. You pay €30-40 for a report (online or at many banks). German landlords expect this. **Without SCHUFA, you're competing at a disadvantage.**

You don't have a SCHUFA score yet as a new arrival; use a **Mietschuldenfreiheitsbescheinigung** (certificate that you have no prior rental debt) from your US landlord, translated.

### 3. Application (Mieterselbstauskunft)
A form asking: who lives here, your income, employment, references, reason for move. Landlords use it to compare applicants.

### 4. Competition
Popular apartments attract 10-30 applicants. **Military families usually compete well**:
- Stable income (government-guaranteed)
- 3-year lease default
- OHA pays Kaltmiete reliably
- No rent arrears risk

### 5. Offer and contract
Landlord chooses. You sign:
- Mietvertrag (contract, 10-20 pages in German)
- Kaution agreement (deposit held in separate bank account, earning interest that's returned to you)
- Inventory list (Übergabeprotokoll) at key handover

### 6. Key handover (Schlüsselübergabe)
- Walk-through with landlord
- Read meter values together
- Receive keys, garage opener, mailbox key
- Signed inventory protocol

## The lease (Mietvertrag)

### Read for:
- **Term**: 1-year, 2-year, or unbefristet (indefinite)
- **Kündigungsfrist** (notice period): usually 3 months
- **Renovation clauses**: must you repaint before moving out? (Many clauses like this are now unenforceable under German law, but read anyway)
- **Pet clauses**: explicitly allowed/disallowed
- **Sub-letting**: typically restricted
- **Rent increase clauses**: Staffelmiete or Indexmiete
- **Haustierhaltung** (pets): often separate paragraph

### HSO RPP standard contract
If you're in the RPP, HSO has templated the contract with SOFA-specific clauses:
- Automatic termination on PCS orders
- Rent in USD / EUR clarity
- Damage deposit reconciliation
- Sublet during deployment permissions

Standard HSO lease is 2-3 years with 30-day break on PCS orders.

## Setting up utilities

### Step by step
1. **Gas / Erdgas**: typically existing account transferred to you. Read meter + submit form.
2. **Electricity / Strom**: same pattern. Choose provider (varies by location; Stadtwerke Wiesbaden is the default).
3. **Water / Wasser**: usually billed through landlord via Nebenkosten; no separate contract
4. **Internet**: you choose (see Internet and Mobile entry)
5. **Rundfunkbeitrag**: auto-starts when you Anmelden

### Timing
Start utility setup **3-5 days before move-in** so your account is active from Day 1. Some utilities have 1-2 week activation lead time.

## Common issues

### Mold (Schimmel)
- Germans take this seriously
- If you see mold in a rental apartment on viewing, walk away or negotiate repair
- If mold appears mid-lease, notify landlord in writing immediately
- Landlord responsibility to fix structural mold; tenant responsibility for ventilation-caused mold

### Noise complaints
- Germans have Ruhezeit (quiet hours) expectations
- Neighbors will complain to Ordnungsamt if you violate
- Fines of €30-300 for persistent noise
- See German Survival Basics entry for detail

### Landlord visits
- Landlord cannot enter without tenant permission under German law (Mieterschutz)
- They can request inspection; you can decline with reasonable cause
- Routine inspections: max twice per year with advance notice

### Rent increases
- German law caps increases (Mietpreisbremse) in some areas
- Existing lease with Kaltmiete cannot be raised by more than 20% in 3 years (15% in Wiesbaden's rent-controlled zones)
- Staffelmiete (scheduled increases) is in the contract; Indexmiete ties to inflation

## End of lease

### Termination (Kündigung)
- Tenant: 3 months written notice typical
- Landlord: protected reasons only (Eigenbedarf - personal use, Mietrückstand - rent arrears)
- **PCS orders qualify as a Sonderkündigung**; immediate termination right for SOFA personnel

### Move-out process
- Written notice 3+ months before departure
- Return keys at Schlüsselübergabe
- Final walk-through with landlord
- Meter readings
- Cleaning (German standard is thorough; hire professional service €200-500 for stress-free)

### Deposit return
- Up to 6 months legal window for landlord to return deposit
- Landlord may withhold for legitimate damages (noted in walk-through)
- If landlord unreasonably holds, small claims court (Amtsgericht) enforces

## Gotchas

- **EBK (built-in kitchen)**; without it, you buy the kitchen. Kitchens cost €2,000-10,000.
- **Neubau vs. Altbau**: new construction vs old building; Altbau is charming but less insulated; heating bills differ
- **Nebenkosten estimated too low**; annual settlement can add €500-1,500
- **Pet friendly** isn't always in writing; confirm explicitly before signing
- **Rent increase rights**; be ready to read German lease closely
- **Roommates / subletting**; usually restricted; written landlord approval required
- **Haftpflichtversicherung (liability insurance)**; mandatory by most landlords; ~€80/year
- **Hausratversicherung (contents insurance)**; not mandatory but expected; ~€80-150/year
- **Parking**: many apartments lack dedicated parking; Stellplatz (parking) adds €50-150/month
- **Renter's rights are strong**: document everything; Germans do
- **Key handover is irreversible**; count keys carefully at move-in; losing one costs €100-500 to re-core

## The RPP path; why most US families use it

Through HSO + RPP:
- Pre-vetted landlords with SOFA-friendly terms
- English or translated contracts
- HSO handles dispute mediation
- Faster approval (you don't compete against 20 Germans)
- Standardized deposit handling
- PCS break clause automatic

Off-post without RPP = competing in open German market. Doable, not recommended unless specific reason.
